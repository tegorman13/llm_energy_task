AI Literacy

Focus: Measuring knowledge, skills, self-perceptions, and competencies regarding AI.

Multi-faceted AI Literacy & Meta-Competencies

üï∏Ô∏è MAILS (Carolus et al., 2023)

34 self-report items assessing perceived abilities across cognitive, technical, ethical, and self-regulatory domains related to AI.

Format: 11-point scale (0=Not at all pronounced, 10=Perfectly pronounced) for "I can..." statements.

Subscales: Use & Apply, Know & Understand, Detect AI, AI Ethics, Create AI, AI Problem-Solving, Learning, Persuasion Literacy, Emotion Regulation.

Example (Know & Understand): "I can assess what the limitations and opportunities of using an AI are." (Scale: 0-10)

Example (AI Ethics): "I can analyze AI-based applications for their ethical implications." (Scale: 0-10)

Brief Multi-faceted AI Literacy Assessment

‚õìÔ∏è MAILS ‚Äì Short Version (Koch et al., 2024)

10-item short form of MAILS covering key competencies.

Format: 11-point scale (0-10) for "I can..." statements.

Subscales: AI Literacy (Detect, Apply, Understand, Ethics), Create AI, AI Self-Efficacy (Learning, Problem-Solving), AI Self-Competency (Persuasion, Emotion Regulation).

Example (Detect AI): "I can tell if I am dealing with an application based on artificial intelligence." (Scale: 0-10)

Example (Create AI): "I can design new AI applications." (Scale: 0-10)

Assesses Factual AI Knowledge

‚úÖ Objective AI Literacy Test (Weber et al., 2023)

16 multiple-choice questions testing objective knowledge across socio-technical user and creator/evaluator dimensions.

Format: Multiple-choice with one correct answer.

Subscales: Socio-User, Socio-Creator/Evaluator, Technical-User, Technical-Creator/Evaluator.

Example (Socio-User): "AI was first mentioned in..." (Options: 1950s, 2000s, 1880s, 1980s)

Example (Technical-Creator): "What is not a strictly necessary part of a single AI system's development process?" (Options: Benchmarking, Training/Learning, Data preprocessing, Model definition)

Objective Knowledge of AI Concepts & Algorithms

üß† AI Literacy Test (Tully et al., 2023)

17 or 25-item multiple-choice test assessing factual understanding of AI concepts, applications, data, algorithms, and ethics.

Format: Multiple-choice with one correct answer.

Subscales: Primarily yields a single knowledge score; potentially factors related to interaction ability, capability understanding, and limitation awareness.

Example: "Which technology is the primary enabler of Artificial Intelligence?" (Options: Machine Learning, Electric Battery, Robotics, Engineering)

Example: "Which of the following is NOT powered by AI?" (Options: Self-driving cars, Google's search algorithm, A basic calculator, Chatbots)

Measures Self-Assessed AI Skills & Knowledge (LLM Context)

üë§ AI Literacy Self-Perception (Rheu & Cho, 2025)

Combines scales measuring self-efficacy, cognitive learning, and specific knowledge about AI technology and processes (steps). Focuses on student context with LLMs.

Format: 7-point Likert scale (1=Strongly disagree, 7=Strongly agree).

Subscales: Self-Efficacy, Cognitive Learning, AI Technology Knowledge, AI-Steps Knowledge.

Example (Self-Efficacy): "I am confident I will perform well on LLM-related tasks." (Scale: 1-7)

Example (AI-Steps Knowledge): "I have knowledge of... The input data requirements for LLMs." (Scale: 1-7)

AI Attitudes & Trust

Focus: Measuring general feelings, specific attitudes, trust dimensions, and beliefs towards AI.

Assesses Overall Positive Attitude Toward AI

üëç General AI Attitude (AIAS-4) (Grassini, 2023)

Brief 4-item scale measuring optimistic beliefs about AI's impact and future use.

Format: 10-point Likert scale (1=Not at all, 10=Completely agree).

Example: "I believe that AI will improve my life." (Scale: 1-10)

Example: "I think AI technology is positive for humanity." (Scale: 1-10)

Measures Acceptance vs. Fear of AI

‚öñÔ∏è Attitude Toward AI (ATAI) (Sindermann et al., 2021)

5-item scale capturing both positive acceptance and negative fear/distrust dimensions of AI attitude.

Format: 11-point Likert scale (0=Strongly disagree, 10=Strongly agree).

Subscales: Acceptance (Trust/Benefit), Fear (Threat/Job Loss).

Example (Acceptance): "I trust artificial intelligence." (Scale: 0-10)

Example (Fear): "I fear artificial intelligence." (Scale: 0-10)

Assesses Cognitive, Affective, Behavioral AI Attitudes

üí¨ Attitudes Towards AI (ATTARI-12) (Stein et al., 2024)

12-item scale measuring general attitudes towards AI across thoughts, feelings, and intentions.

Format: 5-point Likert scale (1=Strongly disagree, 5=Strongly agree). Items mixed positive/negative.

Subscales: Cognitive, Affective, Behavioral.

Example (Cognitive): "AI offers solutions to many world problems." (Scale: 1-5)

Example (Affective): "I am afraid of AI." (Scale: 1-5, reverse-coded)

Measures Dimensions of Trust and Distrust in AI

üõ°Ô∏è Trust Perception Scale - AI (TPA) (Jian et al., 2000; Scharowski et al., 2025)

12-item scale assessing trust and distrust based on perceived deception, suspicion, confidence, reliability, etc.

Format: 7-point scale (1=Not at all, 7=Extremely).

Subscales: Often Trust vs. Distrust factors. Includes a familiarity item.

Example (Distrust): "I am suspicious of the AI's intent, action, or outputs." (Scale: 1-7)

Example (Trust): "The AI is reliable." (Scale: 1-7)

Differentiates Emotional vs. Rational Trust

‚ù§Ô∏èüß† Affective & Cognitive Trust Scale (Shang et al., 2025)

27-item semantic differential scale measuring trust along cognitive (competence, reliability, honesty) and affective (empathy, care, patience) dimensions.

Format: 5-point or 7-point semantic differential scale (-2 to +2 or -3 to +3) using bipolar adjective pairs.

Subscales: Cognitive Trust, Affective Trust.

Example (Cognitive): Unreliable ---(-3)--(-2)--(-1)--(0)--(1)--(2)--(3)--- Reliable

Example (Affective): Apathetic ---(-3)--(-2)--(-1)--(0)--(1)--(2)--(3)--- Empathetic

Scenario-Based Trust Evaluation

üìπ Scharowski Trust Survey (Scharowski et al., 2025)

Uses video vignettes of AI (SDV, Chatbot) in high/low trust scenarios. Measures trust using TPA, single trust/use items, PANAS (affect), risk ratings, and situation-specific trust scales (STS-AD, STS-Chatbot).

Format: Combination of Likert scales (1-7), sliders (0-100), and PANAS emotion ratings (1-5).

Example (Single Item): "I would trust the AI in the scenario." (Scale: 1-7 Agree/Disagree)

Example (STS-AD): "The automated vehicle reacted appropriately to the environment." (Scale: 1-7 Agree/Disagree)

Domain-Specific Attitudes & Experiences

üíºü©∫üéì AI Experience & Attitude Survey (AIEASM) (Gnambs et al., 2025)

Measures attitudes (cognitive, affective, behavioral) within Work, Healthcare, and Education domains. Also assesses awareness, positivity, past experience, and future usage intention for specific AI scenarios (virtual assistants, recommenders, robots, etc.).

Format: Likert scales (0-4 agreement, 0-10 positivity) and frequency/experience scales (0-4).

Example (Work Attitude): "Artificial intelligence offers good solutions for many job tasks." (Scale: 0-4 Agree/Disagree)

Example (Scenario Experience): "How much personal experience have you had with [Care robots] in the last 12 months?" (Scale: 0-4 None to Very Much)

European Public AI Awareness, Attitude & Trust

üá™üá∫ Perceptions on AI by Citizens of Europe (PAICE) (Scantamburlo et al., 2025)

Broad survey covering self-rated competency, general attitude, perceived impact, awareness of AI apps/initiatives, comfort with scenarios, importance of trust measures, and trust in various entities (Govt, EU, Companies, etc.).

Format: Likert scales (1-5), Yes/No, Multiple Choice (select/rank).

Example (Attitude): "How would you describe your attitude towards Artificial Intelligence (AI) and its applications?" (Scale: 1-5 Strongly disapprove to Strongly approve)

Example (Trust): "How much do you trust [Universities and research centres] in ensuring that AI is in the best interest of the public?" (Scale: 1-5 Not at all to A lot)

LLM Perceptions

Focus: Specific perceptions, beliefs, and evaluations related to Large Language Models.

Assesses Attributed Mental States to LLMs

üí≠ Mental Capacity Attribution (Chen et al., 2025)

Measures the extent to which people attribute human-like mental capacities (related to body, heart, mind) to LLMs.

Format: 7-point capability scale (1=Not at all capable, 7=Highly capable).

Example (Heart): "...understanding how others are feeling?" (Scale: 1-7)

Example (Mind): "...having intentions?" (Scale: 1-7)

Measures Belief in AI vs. Other Predictions

üîÆ Prediction Believability & AI Superstition (Lee et al., 2024)

Compares the believability (validity, personalization, reliability, usefulness) of behavioral predictions attributed to AI, Astrology, and Personality tests. Investigates psychological factors influencing belief.

Format: 7-point Likert scale (1=Strongly disagree, 7=Strongly agree) for evaluating predictions. Also includes CRT, NCS-6, AIAS-4, Gullibility, Paranormal Beliefs scales.

Example (Validity): "I find the [AI] prediction convincing." (Scale: 1-7)

Example (Usefulness): "The [Astrology] prediction is useful for making future decisions." (Scale: 1-7)

Evaluates Perceived Empathy of LLM Responses

ü´∂ Perceived LLM Compassion/Responsiveness (Ovsyannikova et al., 2025)

Compares perceived compassion and responsiveness of LLM (ChatGPT-4) vs. human responses to emotional prompts.

Format: 5-point Likert scales for compassion facets. Binary choice for preference. 5-point Likert for responsiveness facets.

Example (Compassion): Rate response on how much it... "was compassionate" (Scale: 1-5 Disagree/Agree)

Example (Preference): "Which one of the two responses was better...?" (Options: Human, AI)

Measures Perceptions of LLMs as Expert/Objective/Accurate

ü§ñ Machine Heuristics for LLMs (Rheu & Cho, 2025)

Assesses the extent to which users perceive LLMs (like ChatGPT) as knowledgeable, unbiased, and precise.

Format: 7-point Likert scale (1=Strongly disagree, 7=Strongly agree).

Subscales: Expert, Objective, Accurate.

Example (Objective): "LLMs applications are... Impartial" (Scale: 1-7)

Example (Accurate): "LLMs applications are... Exact" (Scale: 1-7)

Infers User Goals & Needs from Text (LLM Application)

üßò Empathic Mental Inference from User Text (Zhu et al., 2024)

Task where LLMs (or humans) infer user goals from product reviews and attribute them to psychological needs.

Format: Open-ended goal inference. 5-point Likert scale for attributing goals to 13 fundamental needs.

Example (Goal Inference): "Based on the product comment... what are your overarching goals...?" (Open text)

Example (Need Attribution): Attribute inferred goals to "Security" (Scale: 1=Not attributed at all, 5=Highly attributed)

Assesses Social Influence of Multi-Agent LLMs

üßë‚Äçü§ù‚Äçüßë Social Influence & Agent Impressions (Song et al., 2024)

Measures opinion change after interacting with single or multiple AI agents. Assesses perceived influence and user impressions.

Format: Likert scales (6 or 7-point) for opinion, influence, impressions. Open-ended questions.

Subscales: User Opinion, Informational Influence, Normative Influence, Agent Impressions.

Example (Informational Influence): "My decision was influenced by the opinion of the agent(s)." (Scale: 1-7)

Example (Agent Impression): "The agent(s) were knowledgeable and had topic expertise." (Scale: 1-7)

Energy Literacy

Focus: Measuring knowledge, perceptions, attitudes, and context related to energy use.

Measures Knowledge & Perception of Energy Consumption/Savings

üí° Energy Literacy Assessment (Attari et al., 2010)

Assesses perceived effectiveness, estimates of household energy breakdown, relative energy use of devices, relative savings, attitudes (NEP), and behaviors.

Format: Open-ended, percentage estimation, numeric estimation relative to baseline, Likert scales (NEP).

Example (Estimation): "Energy Consumed by Average Household - Transportation (%)" (Numeric Input)

Example (Relative Use): "Energy Used by Devices in One Hour - A desktop computer" (Numeric Input relative to 100 units)

Comprehensive Assessment of Knowledge, Affect, Behavior

üìö Energy Literacy Questionnaire (DeWaters & Powers, 2011; DeWaters et al., 2013)

Measures factual knowledge (cognitive), attitudes/values/efficacy (affective), and conservation actions (behavioral) related to energy.

Format: Multiple-choice (cognitive), 5-point Likert (affective/behavioral).

Subscales: Cognitive, Affective, Behavioral.

Example (Cognitive): "The amount of ELECTRICAL ENERGY (ELECTRICITY) we use is measured in units called ..." (MC Options)

Example (Affective): "Saving energy is important." (Scale: 1-5 Agree/Disagree)

Investigates & Corrects Energy Estimation Biases

‚öôÔ∏è Energy Use Misperceptions & Interventions (Marghetis et al., 2019)

Uses estimation tasks, choice tasks, numeracy/CRT, and attitude scales (NEP) to assess misperceptions and intervention impacts.

Format: Numeric estimation, binary choice, multiple-choice, Likert scales.

Example (Estimation): Estimate Wh for "Clothes dryer" (Numeric Input)

Example (Choice Task): "Which uses less energy? (Left) Using an electric blanket for an hour OR (Right) Using a space heater for an hour." (Binary Choice)

Explores How Experts Judge Energy Use

üßë‚Äçüî¨ Expert Energy Heuristics (Kantenbacher & Attari, 2021)

Uses talk-aloud protocols during choice/estimation tasks to identify expert heuristics. Includes survey rating accuracy of heuristic rules.

Format: Talk-aloud, choice tasks, estimation tasks, Likert scale for heuristic accuracy (1-4).

Example (Heuristic Rating): "Devices with heating elements use a lot of energy." (Scale: 1-4 Accuracy)

Evaluates Understanding & Preference for Energy Reports

üìä Electricity-Use Communications Perceptions (Canfield et al., 2017)

Measures understanding of different energy report formats (T/F questions). Assesses preferences (clarity, usefulness, trust) and intentions (Likert).

Format: True/False, 7-point Likert scales.

Example (Understanding): "True / False: The Smith's home used more electricity in February 2011 than February 2012."

Example (Preference): "How clear is this information?" (Scale: 1=Not at all, 7=Very much)

Measures Attitudes towards Energy Issues

üåç Energy-Issue Attitude Questionnaire (Mei-Shiu et al., 2018)

Assesses attitudes towards energy problems, solutions, and policies. (Specific items in linked PDF).

Format: Likert scale (assumed).

Assesses Knowledge & Attitudes towards EVs & Heat Pumps

üöó‚ô®Ô∏è Low Carbon Technology Narratives (Attari et al., 2025)

Measures familiarity, ownership, barriers, motivations, and knowledge (T/F) regarding EVs and heat pumps. Includes climate attitudes.

Format: Likert scales, Yes/No, True/False, Open-ended text.

Example (Knowledge): "Heat pumps can both heat and cool my home." (True/False)

Example (Barrier): "What is the biggest barrier preventing you from buying or leasing an electric vehicle?" (Open Text)

Measures Climate Beliefs & Related Psych Traits

‚öñÔ∏èüçÉ Environmental Decision-Making Context (He et al., 2024)

Measures climate change beliefs/concerns, competitiveness, risk/time preferences, loss aversion, and experience with energy bill social comparisons.

Format: Likert scales (1-7), 0-10 scales, Yes/No.

Example (Climate Concern): "Considering any potential effects of climate change on society in general, how concerned are you about climate change?" (Scale: 1-7)

Example (Experience): "Have you ever received information about how your consumption/behaviour compares to... comparable households?" (Yes/No)

Numeracy

Focus: Measuring numerical literacy and cognitive reflection.

Assesses Understanding of Probability/Percentages

üßÆ Statistical Numeracy (Attari et al. 2010 / Berlin Numeracy Test)

Uses probability problems (e.g., coin flips, lottery chances, disease risk) to assess statistical numeracy and risk literacy.

Format: Typically open numeric entry or multiple-choice.

Example (BNT): "Imagine we are tossing a fair coin 1,000 times. What is your best guess about how many times the coin would come up heads...?" (Numeric Input)

Example (BNT): "Which of the following numbers represents the biggest risk of getting a disease? 1 in 100, 1 in 1000, 1 in 10" (Multiple Choice)

Measures Tendency to Override Intuition

‚ùì Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) (Various Studies)

Assesses propensity to engage in reflective thinking over intuitive responses using classic brain teaser-style problems.

Format: Open numeric entry or forced choice between common incorrect and correct answer.

Example (CRT): "A bat and a ball cost $1.10 in total. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost? (in cents)" (Numeric Input: Correct=5)

Example (CRT-2): "If you're running a race and you pass the person in second place, what place are you in?" (Options: first, second)

Behavioral Measures

Focus: Assessing self-reported behaviors, intentions, and related psychological constructs.

Measures Frequency of Energy-Saving Actions

‚ôªÔ∏è Energy Conservation Behaviors (DeWaters & Powers, 2011)

Assesses how often individuals perform common energy conservation actions.

Format: 5-point Likert frequency scale (e.g., 1=Never to 5=Almost Always).

Example: "When I leave a room, I turn off the lights." (Scale: 1-5 Frequency)

Assesses Perceived Ease/Difficulty of Adopting Behaviors

üí™ Perceived Difficulty of Energy Behaviors (Attari et al., 2010)

Measures how hard or easy participants perceive making specific energy-saving changes to be.

Format: Categorical scale including "Do it already", "Extremely easy" through "Extremely hard", and "Not applicable".

Example: "Carpooling with one other person to work" (Categorical Response)

Measures Information Verification & Critical Evaluation with AI

üîé AI Fact-Checking / Critical Thinking (Rheu & Cho, 2025; Gerlich, 2025)

Assesses how often users verify information from AI/LLMs or engage in critical thinking when using these tools.

Format: Likert frequency scales (e.g., 1=Never to 7=Always or 1-6 Disagree/Agree).

Example (Rheu & Cho): "Use other search engines to further research on the topic [from LLM]." (Scale: 1-7 Frequency)

Example (Gerlich): "I often cross-check information provided by AI tools with other sources." (Scale: 1-6 Agree/Disagree)

Assesses Potential Overreliance & Negative Use Patterns

‚ö†Ô∏è Problematic AI/Chatbot Use & Dependence (Fang et al., 2025; Gerlich, 2025)

Measures aspects like preoccupation, withdrawal, loss of control, neglect of activities, and emotional reliance related to AI/chatbot use.

Format: 5-point Likert agreement scale (1=Disagree, 5=Agree).

Example (Fang - PCUS): "I experience anxiety or irritability when unable to access the chatbot." (Scale: 1-5)

Example (Fang - ADS-9): "I think I am emotionally dependent on the chatbot." (Scale: 1-5)

Example (Gerlich - Reliance): "I find myself relying heavily on AI tools for tasks I used to do myself." (Scale: 1-6)

Measures Tendency to Delegate Cognitive Tasks to Tech

‚òÅÔ∏è‚¨ÜÔ∏è Cognitive Offloading (Gerlich, 2025)

Assesses the frequency of using digital tools (search engines, devices) to store information or solve problems instead of relying on internal memory or effort.

Format: 6-point Likert frequency/agreement scale.

Example: "How often do you use search engines like Google to retrieve information you have looked up before?" (Scale: 1-6 Frequency)

Includes scales like Big Five, Dark Triad, Need for Cognition, Paranormal Beliefs, Gullibility, Competitiveness, Loss Aversion, Compliance, Conformity, etc.

Format: Various Likert scales (typically 5 or 7 points).
