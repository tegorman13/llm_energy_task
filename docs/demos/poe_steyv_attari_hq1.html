Overview: Cognitive Biases in Energy Perception and AI Confidence

Attari et al. (2010)

Public Perceptions of Energy Consumption and Savings

This landmark study reveals that people systematically compress the true distribution of energy magnitudes:

  • Overestimating energy use of low-consumption activities
  • Underestimating energy use of high-consumption activities
  • The average compression factor is about 2.8
  • Focus on curtailment rather than efficiency improvements

AI Confidence Calibration Concept

The Calibration Gap in AI Explanations

Note: Conceptual framework based on emerging research

  • Misalignment between AI internal confidence and human perception
  • Longer explanations increase human confidence without improving accuracy
  • Linguistic markers of uncertainty/confidence significantly impact trust
  • A "discrimination gap" where humans struggle to identify AI errors

Cognitive Connections: Parallel Biases Across Domains

Both areas of research reveal similar systematic biases in human perception and judgment:

Shared Cognitive Mechanisms

Compression Flattening the true range of values, leading to overestimation of small values and underestimation of large ones
Anchoring Starting from reference points and making insufficient adjustments
Availability Giving disproportionate weight to easily recalled examples
Overconfidence Being more confident than is warranted by actual accuracy

Impact of AI Advice on Energy Perception

Methodology

Attari et al. (2010) Methodology

Participants and Design

  • 505 participants completed an online survey
  • Estimated energy used by 9 devices/appliances and saved by 6 activities
  • A 100-watt incandescent light bulb used for 1 hour provided as a reference point

Data Analysis

  • Log-transformed both perceived and actual energy values to reduce skew
  • Calculated correlation between perceived and actual energy
  • Used multilevel regression to model relationship between perceptions and actual values
  • Assessed individual differences (numeracy, environmental attitudes)

Key Regression Equation

log10Perceptionij = β0j + β1jlog10Actuali + β2j(log10Actuali)2 + rij

Where i indicates device/activity, j indicates participant, β0j is elevation (underestimation), β1j is slope (compression), and β2j is quadratic term (curvature).

AI Confidence Calibration Methodology

Experimental Design

  • Multiple experiments with online participants judging LLM answers
  • Used multiple LLMs: GPT-3.5, PaLM2, and GPT-4o
  • Multiple-choice and short-answer question formats
  • Manipulated explanation style (uncertain, moderate, confident)
  • Manipulated explanation length (short, medium, long)

Measuring Confidence

  • Model confidence assessed through token probabilities
  • Multiple-choice questions: normalized probabilities across answer options
  • Short-answer questions: "pTrue" method asking model to evaluate its own answer
  • Human confidence measured through explicit confidence ratings

Expected Calibration Error (ECE)

ECE = Σm=1M |Bm|/N · |conf(Bm) - acc(Bm)|

Measures calibration by comparing confidence and accuracy across probability bins.

Area Under Curve (AUC)

AUC = 1/(Npos × Nneg) · Σi=1Npos Σj=1Nneg I(Ci > Cj)

Measures discrimination ability by comparing confidence scores for correct vs. incorrect answers.

Methodological Connections

Both studies employ similar approaches to investigate perception biases:

Shared Methodological Elements

  • Reference Points: Both provide anchors for judgments (100W light bulb for energy; explanation formats for AI)
  • Manipulation of Key Variables: Both systematically vary factors that affect perception
  • Logarithmic Transformations: Both use log transformations to analyze skewed distributions
  • Quantitative Analysis: Both use statistical methods to model the relationship between actual and perceived values
  • Individual Differences: Both examine how personal characteristics affect calibration

Implications and Applications

Implications for Energy Communication

  • Energy campaigns should focus on high-impact actions that are typically underestimated
  • Communication about efficiency improvements (vs. curtailment) should be prioritized
  • Use familiar reference points to help contextualize energy magnitudes
  • Consider using log or percentage scales rather than absolute values
  • Tailored feedback can help correct individual compression biases

Implications for AI Design

  • AI explanations should reflect the model's actual confidence levels
  • Avoid lengthy explanations for low-confidence answers
  • Use appropriate linguistic markers of uncertainty
  • Design UIs that explicitly communicate confidence levels
  • Train users to better interpret AI confidence signals

Combined Applications for AI Energy Advisors

Adaptive Explanations

AI energy advisors should provide more detailed explanations for high-energy activities that humans systematically underestimate, while using confidence language that matches the model's actual certainty.

Calibration Feedback

Systems should provide feedback on user estimates, helping to correct compression bias while clearly distinguishing between high-confidence factual information and lower-confidence recommendations.

Personalized Visualization

Create personalized dashboards that adjust visual scales and reference points based on the user's specific compression factor, while presenting AI confidence in ways that facilitate appropriate trust.

Future Research Directions

  • Investigate how AI advice differentially affects perceptions of high vs. low energy activities
  • Test whether appropriately calibrated AI explanations can help correct human compression effects
  • Explore how domain expertise affects reliance on AI advice with varying confidence expressions
  • Design interfaces that adjust explanation style based on both the AI's confidence and the magnitude of energy use
  • Study how these cognitive biases might interact in other domains like financial planning or health risk assessment
