Focus: Measuring knowledge, skills, self-perceptions, and competencies regarding AI

Network üï∏Ô∏è Multi-faceted AI Literacy & Meta-Competencies

MAILS (Carolus et al., 2023)
  • Description: 34 self-report items assessing perceived abilities across cognitive, technical, ethical, and self-regulatory domains.
  • Format: 11-point scale (0-10) for ‚ÄúI can‚Ä¶‚Äù statements.
  • Subscales: Use & Apply, Know & Understand, Detect AI, AI Ethics, Create AI, AI Problem-Solving, Learning, Persuasion Literacy, Emotion Regulation.
  • Example (Know & Understand): ‚ÄúI can assess what the limitations and opportunities of using an AI are.‚Äù (0-10)

Network (Short) ‚õìÔ∏è Brief Multi-faceted AI Literacy Assessment

MAILS ‚Äì Short Version (Koch et al., 2024)
  • Description: 10-item short form covering key competencies.
  • Format: 11-point scale (0-10) for ‚ÄúI can‚Ä¶‚Äù statements.
  • Subscales: AI Literacy (Detect, Apply, Understand, Ethics), Create AI, AI Self-Efficacy, AI Self-Competency.
  • Example (Detect AI): ‚ÄúI can tell if I am dealing with an application based on artificial intelligence.‚Äù (0-10)

Checkmark ‚úÖ Assesses Factual AI Knowledge

Objective AI Literacy Test (Weber et al., 2023)
  • Description: 16 multiple-choice questions testing objective knowledge (socio-technical user/creator dimensions).
  • Format: Multiple-choice (one correct answer).
  • Subscales: Socio-User, Socio-Creator/Evaluator, Technical-User, Technical-Creator/Evaluator.
  • Example (Socio-User): ‚ÄúAI was first mentioned in‚Ä¶‚Äù (1950s)

Brain üß† Objective Knowledge of AI Concepts & Algorithms

AI Literacy Test (Tully et al., 2023)
  • Description: 17 or 25-item multiple-choice test (concepts, applications, data, algorithms, ethics).
  • Format: Multiple-choice (one correct answer).
  • Subscales: Single score; potential factors (interaction, capability, limitation).
  • Example: ‚ÄúWhich technology is the primary enabler of Artificial Intelligence?‚Äù (Machine Learning)

User Profileüë§ Measures Self-Assessed AI Skills & Knowledge (LLM Context)

AI Literacy Self-Perception (Rheu & Cho, 2025)
  • Description: Scales for self-efficacy, cognitive learning, AI tech/steps knowledge (student LLM context).
  • Format: 7-point Likert scale (1-7 Agree/Disagree).
  • Subscales: Self-Efficacy, Cognitive Learning, AI Technology Knowledge, AI-Steps Knowledge.
  • Example (Self-Efficacy): ‚ÄúI am confident I will perform well on LLM-related tasks.‚Äù (1-7)

Focus: Measuring general feelings, specific attitudes, trust dimensions, and beliefs towards AI

Thumbs Up üëç Assesses Overall Positive Attitude Toward AI

General AI Attitude (AIAS-4) (Grassini, 2023)
  • Description: Brief 4-item scale measuring optimistic beliefs about AI‚Äôs impact.
  • Format: 10-point Likert scale (1-10 Agree/Disagree).
  • Example: ‚ÄúI believe that AI will improve my life.‚Äù (1-10)

Balance Scale ‚öñÔ∏è Measures Acceptance vs. Fear of AI

Attitude Toward AI (ATAI) (Sindermann et al., 2021)
  • Description: 5-item scale capturing acceptance and fear/distrust dimensions.
  • Format: 11-point Likert scale (0-10 Agree/Disagree).
  • Subscales: Acceptance (Trust/Benefit), Fear (Threat/Job Loss).
  • Example (Acceptance): ‚ÄúI trust artificial intelligence.‚Äù (0-10)

Speech Bubbleüí¨ Assesses Cognitive, Affective, Behavioral AI Attitudes

Attitudes Towards AI (ATTARI-12) (Stein et al., 2024)
  • Description: 12-item scale (thoughts, feelings, intentions).
  • Format: 5-point Likert scale (1-5 Agree/Disagree). Mixed positive/negative.
  • Subscales: Cognitive, Affective, Behavioral.
  • Example (Cognitive): ‚ÄúAI offers solutions to many world problems.‚Äù (1-5)

Shield üõ°Ô∏è Measures Dimensions of Trust and Distrust in AI

Trust Perception Scale - AI (TPA) (Jian et al., 2000; Scharowski et al., 2025)
  • Description: 12-item scale (deception, suspicion, confidence, reliability).
  • Format: 7-point scale (1=Not at all, 7=Extremely).
  • Subscales: Trust vs. Distrust factors. Includes familiarity.
  • Example (Distrust): ‚ÄúI am suspicious of the AI‚Äôs intent, action, or outputs.‚Äù (1-7)

Heart & Brain ‚ù§Ô∏èüß† Differentiates Emotional vs. Rational Trust

Affective & Cognitive Trust Scale (Shang et al., 2025)
  • Description: 27-item semantic differential scale (cognitive: competence, reliability, honesty; affective: empathy, care, patience).
  • Format: 5 or 7-point semantic differential (-2/+2 or -3/+3).
  • Subscales: Cognitive Trust, Affective Trust.
  • Example (Cognitive): Unreliable ---(-3)--(-2)--(-1)--(0)--(1)--(2)--(3)--- Reliable

Video Camera üìπ Scenario-Based Trust Evaluation

Scharowski Trust Survey (Scharowski et al., 2025)
  • Description: Uses video vignettes (SDV, Chatbot) in high/low trust scenarios. Measures trust (TPA, single items, STS-AD/Chatbot), affect (PANAS), risk.
  • Format: Likert (1-7), sliders (0-100), PANAS (1-5).
  • Example (Single Item): ‚ÄúI would trust the AI in the scenario.‚Äù (1-7 Agree/Disagree)

Briefcase üíº / Stethoscope ü©∫ / Cap üéì Domain-Specific Attitudes & Experiences

AI Experience & Attitude Survey (AIEASM) (Gnambs et al., 2025)
  • Description: Attitudes (cognitive, affective, behavioral) in Work, Healthcare, Education. Assesses awareness, positivity, experience, intention for specific AI scenarios.
  • Format: Likert (0-4 agreement, 0-10 positivity), frequency/experience (0-4).
  • Example (Work Attitude): ‚ÄúArtificial intelligence offers good solutions for many job tasks.‚Äù (0-4 Agree/Disagree)

EU Flag üá™üá∫ European Public AI Awareness, Attitude & Trust

Perceptions on AI by Citizens of Europe (PAICE) (Scantamburlo et al., 2025)
  • Description: Broad survey: self-rated competency, attitude, impact, awareness, comfort, trust importance, trust in entities.
  • Format: Likert (1-5), Y/N, MC (select/rank).
  • Example (Attitude): ‚ÄúHow would you describe your attitude towards Artificial Intelligence (AI)‚Ä¶?‚Äù (1-5 Disapprove/Approve)

Focus: Specific perceptions, beliefs, and evaluations related to Large Language Models

Thought Bubble üí≠ Assesses Attributed Mental States to LLMs

Mental Capacity Attribution (Chen et al., 2025)
  • Description: Measures attribution of human-like mental capacities (body, heart, mind) to LLMs.
  • Format: 7-point capability scale (1=Not capable, 7=Highly capable).
  • Example (Heart): ‚Äú‚Ä¶understanding how others are feeling?‚Äù (1-7)

Crystal Ball üîÆ Measures Belief in AI vs. Other Predictions

Prediction Believability & AI Superstition (Lee et al., 2024)
  • Description: Compares believability (validity, personalization, reliability, usefulness) of predictions (AI, Astrology, Personality tests). Includes psych factors (CRT, NCS, AIAS, Gullibility, Paranormal).
  • Format: 7-point Likert (1-7 Agree/Disagree) for predictions; various scales for factors.
  • Example (Validity): ‚ÄúI find the [AI] prediction convincing.‚Äù (1-7)

Heart Handsü´∂ Evaluates Perceived Empathy of LLM Responses

Perceived LLM Compassion/Responsiveness (Ovsyannikova et al., 2025)
  • Description: Compares perceived compassion/responsiveness of LLM vs. human responses to emotional prompts.
  • Format: 5-point Likert (compassion/responsiveness facets); Binary choice (preference).
  • Example (Compassion): Rate response on how much it‚Ä¶ ‚Äúwas compassionate‚Äù (1-5 Disagree/Agree)

Robot ü§ñ Measures Perceptions of LLMs as Expert/Objective/Accurate

Machine Heuristics for LLMs (Rheu & Cho, 2025)
  • Description: Assesses perception of LLMs as knowledgeable, unbiased, precise.
  • Format: 7-point Likert scale (1-7 Agree/Disagree).
  • Subscales: Expert, Objective, Accurate.
  • Example (Objective): ‚ÄúLLMs applications are‚Ä¶ Impartial‚Äù (1-7)

Mind Reading üßò Infers User Goals & Needs from Text (LLM Application)

Empathic Mental Inference from User Text (Zhu et al., 2024)
  • Description: Task inferring user goals (task, sub-action, overarching) from text and attributing to fundamental needs.
  • Format: Open-ended goal inference; 5-point Likert for need attribution (13 needs).
  • Example (Need Attribution): Attribute inferred goals to ‚ÄúSecurity‚Äù (1=Not attributed, 5=Highly attributed)

Group Chat üßë‚Äçü§ù‚Äçüßë Assesses Social Influence of Multi-Agent LLMs

Social Influence & Agent Impressions (Song et al., 2024)
  • Description: Measures opinion change after interacting with single/multiple AI agents. Assesses informational/normative influence, agent impressions.
  • Format: Likert scales (6/7-point) for opinion, influence, impressions; Open-ended questions.
  • Subscales: Opinion, Info Influence, Norm Influence, Agent Impressions.
  • Example (Info Influence): ‚ÄúMy decision was influenced by the opinion of the agent(s).‚Äù (1-7)

Focus: Measuring knowledge, perceptions, attitudes, and context related to energy use

Lightbulb üí° Measures Knowledge & Perception of Energy Consumption/Savings

Energy Literacy Assessment (Attari et al., 2010)
  • Description: Assesses perceived savings effectiveness, household breakdown (%), relative device use (vs. 100W bulb), relative savings, attitudes (NEP), behaviors.
  • Format: Open-ended, %, numeric estimation, Likert (NEP).
  • Example (Estimation): ‚ÄúEnergy Consumed by Average Household - Transportation (%)‚Äù (Numeric Input)

Textbook üìö Comprehensive Assessment of Knowledge, Affect, Behavior

Energy Literacy Questionnaire (DeWaters et al., 2013)
  • Description: Measures factual knowledge (cognitive), attitudes/values/efficacy (affective), conservation actions (behavioral) for secondary students.
  • Format: MC (cognitive), 5-point Likert (affective/behavioral).
  • Subscales: Cognitive, Affective, Behavioral.
  • Example (Cognitive): ‚ÄúThe amount of ELECTRICAL ENERGY‚Ä¶ is measured in‚Ä¶‚Äù (MC Options)

Brain with Gears ‚öôÔ∏è Investigates & Corrects Energy Estimation Biases

Energy Use Misperceptions & Interventions (Marghetis et al., 2019)
  • Description: Uses estimation (36 appliances vs. 100W bulb), choice tasks, numeracy/CRT, attitudes (NEP) to assess misperceptions and intervention impact.
  • Format: Numeric estimation, binary choice, MC, Likert.
  • Example (Estimation): Estimate Wh for ‚ÄúClothes dryer‚Äù (Numeric Input)

Scientist üßë‚Äçüî¨ Explores How Experts Judge Energy Use

Expert Energy Heuristics (Kantenbacher & Attari, 2021)
  • Description: Uses talk-aloud during choice/estimation to identify expert heuristics. Includes rating accuracy of predefined rules.
  • Format: Talk-aloud, choice/estimation tasks, Likert (1-4 Accuracy).
  • Example (Heuristic Rating): ‚ÄúDevices with heating elements use a lot of energy.‚Äù (1-4 Accuracy)

Bar Chart üìä Evaluates Understanding & Preference for Energy Reports

Electricity-Use Communications Perceptions (Canfield et al., 2017)
  • Description: Measures understanding (T/F) of report formats (history, neighbor, appliance). Assesses preferences (clarity, usefulness, trust), intentions (Likert). Includes energy literacy subscale.
  • Format: T/F, 7-point Likert.
  • Example (Understanding): ‚ÄúTrue / False: The Smith‚Äôs home used more electricity in Feb 2011 than Feb 2012.‚Äù

Globe üåç Measures Attitudes towards Energy Issues

Energy-Issue Attitude Questionnaire (Mei-Shiu et al., 2018)
  • Description: Assesses attitudes towards energy problems, solutions, policies. Developed to relate attitudes to knowledge, affect, behavior. (Note: Specific .md file not identified in provided list).
  • Format: Likert scale (assumed).
  • (Items available in source PDF)

EV Car üöó / Heat Pump ‚ô®Ô∏è Assesses Knowledge & Attitudes towards EVs & Heat Pumps

Low Carbon Technology Narratives (Attari et al., 2025)
  • Description: Measures familiarity, ownership, barriers, motivations, knowledge (T/F) for EVs/heat pumps. Includes climate attitudes.
  • Format: Likert, Y/N, T/F, Open-ended.
  • Example (Knowledge): ‚ÄúHeat pumps can both heat and cool my home.‚Äù (T/F)

Scales ‚öñÔ∏è / Leaf üçÉ Measures Climate Beliefs & Related Psych Traits

Environmental Decision-Making Context (He et al., 2024)
  • Description: Measures climate beliefs/concerns, competitiveness, risk/time preferences, loss aversion, experience with energy social comparisons.
  • Format: Likert (1-7), 0-10 scales, Y/N.
  • Example (Climate Concern): ‚Äú‚Ä¶how concerned are you about climate change?‚Äù (1-7 Not to Strongly concerned)

Focus: Measuring numerical literacy and cognitive reflection

Calculator üßÆ Assesses Understanding of Probability/Percentages

Statistical Numeracy (Attari et al. 2010 / Berlin Numeracy Test)
  • Links: Berlin Numeracy Test Items, Numeracy in Attari 2010
  • Description: Uses probability problems (coin flips, lottery, disease risk) to assess statistical numeracy and risk literacy.
  • Format: Open numeric entry or multiple-choice.
  • Example (BNT): ‚ÄúImagine we are tossing a fair coin 1,000 times‚Ä¶ heads‚Ä¶?‚Äù (Numeric Input)

Question Mark‚ùì Measures Tendency to Override Intuition

Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) (Various Studies)
  • Links: Used in Lee et al. 2024, Used in Marghetis et al. 2019
  • Description: Assesses propensity for reflective thinking over intuitive responses using brain teaser problems.
  • Format: Open numeric entry or forced choice.
  • Example (CRT): ‚ÄúA bat and a ball cost $1.10‚Ä¶ The bat costs $1.00 more‚Ä¶ How much does the ball cost?‚Äù (Numeric Input: Correct=5 cents)

Focus: Assessing self-reported behaviors, intentions, and related psychological constructs

Recycle ‚ôªÔ∏è Measures Frequency of Energy-Saving Actions

Energy Conservation Behaviors (DeWaters & Powers, 2011)
  • Description: Assesses how often individuals perform common energy conservation actions.
  • Format: 5-point Likert frequency scale (1=Never to 5=Almost Always).
  • Item Example: ‚ÄúWhen I leave a room, I turn off the lights.‚Äù (1-5 Frequency)

Weightlifter üí™ Assesses Perceived Ease/Difficulty of Adopting Behaviors

Perceived Difficulty of Energy Behaviors (Attari et al., 2010)
  • Description: Measures perceived difficulty of making specific energy-saving changes.
  • Format: Categorical scale (‚ÄúDo it already‚Äù, ‚ÄúExtremely easy‚Äù to ‚ÄúExtremely hard‚Äù, ‚ÄúN/A‚Äù).
  • Item Example: ‚ÄúCarpooling with one other person to work‚Äù (Categorical Response)

Magnifying Glass üîé Measures Information Verification & Critical Evaluation with AI

AI Fact-Checking / Critical Thinking
  • Links: Rheu & Cho, 2025, Gerlich, 2025
  • Description: Assesses frequency of verifying AI/LLM info or engaging in critical thinking.
  • Format: Likert frequency scales (e.g., 1-7 Always/Never or 1-6 Agree/Disagree).
  • Example (Rheu): ‚ÄúUse other search engines to further research‚Ä¶‚Äù (1-7 Frequency)
  • Example (Gerlich): ‚ÄúI often cross-check information provided by AI tools‚Ä¶‚Äù (1-6 Agree/Disagree)

Warning Sign ‚ö†Ô∏è Assesses Potential Overreliance & Negative Use Patterns

Problematic AI/Chatbot Use & Dependence
  • Links: Fang et al., 2025, Gerlich, 2025
  • Description: Measures preoccupation, withdrawal, loss of control, neglect, emotional reliance on AI/chatbots.
  • Format: 5-point Likert agreement scale (1=Disagree, 5=Agree).
  • Example (Fang - PCUS): ‚ÄúI experience anxiety‚Ä¶ when unable to access the chatbot.‚Äù (1-5)
  • Example (Gerlich - Reliance): ‚ÄúI find myself relying heavily on AI tools‚Ä¶‚Äù (1-6)

Cloud Upload ‚òÅÔ∏è‚¨ÜÔ∏è Measures Tendency to Delegate Cognitive Tasks to Tech

Cognitive Offloading (Gerlich, 2025)
  • Description: Assesses frequency of using digital tools (search, devices) instead of internal memory/effort.
  • Format: 6-point Likert frequency/agreement scale.
  • Item Example: ‚ÄúHow often do you use search engines‚Ä¶ to retrieve information you have looked up before?‚Äù (1-6 Frequency)

Person Puzzle üß© Measures Individual Differences Correlated with Behavior

Personality, Beliefs & Other Traits (Various Studies)
  • Links: See studies like Lee et al. 2024, Stein et al. 2024, Gnambs et al. 2025, He et al. 2024
  • Description: Includes scales like Big Five (BFI/TIPI), Dark Triad (SD3), Need for Cognition (NCS-6), Paranormal Beliefs (R-PBS), Gullibility, Competitiveness, Loss Aversion, Compliance, Conformity, etc.
  • Format: Various Likert scales (typically 5 or 7 points).
